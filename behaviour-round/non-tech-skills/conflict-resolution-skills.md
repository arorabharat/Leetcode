# Describe a situation where teams had conflicting priorities. How did you align them?

In behavioral interviews, this question is designed to test your **conflict resolution, negotiation, and leadership skills**. The key is to show you can move from "me vs. them" to "us vs. the goal."

Here are five variations of how to structure your response using different professional frameworks.

---

## 1. The STAR Method (The Standard)
**Best for:** A straightforward, logical narrative of a specific event.
* **Situation:** Set the scene. "Team A wanted to launch a new feature to hit a Q3 goal, but the DevOps team prioritized a critical security patch."
* **Task:** Define the conflict. "I needed to ensure the feature launched without compromising system integrity."
* **Action:** What did *you* do? "I facilitated a data-sync where both teams presented their risks. We mapped out a hybrid timeline."
* **Result:** The outcome. "The patch was applied on Monday, and the feature launched by Thursday with zero downtime."

## 2. The Interest-Based Relational (IBR) Approach
**Best for:** Situations involving high emotions or strained professional relationships.
* **Prioritize Relationships:** Start by acknowledging you kept the atmosphere professional and calm.
* **Separate People from Problems:** Explain how you focused on the *priorities*, not the people holding them.
* **Listen to All Interests:** "I sat down with both leads to understand the 'why' behind their urgency."
* **Brainstorm "Win-Win":** Describe the collaborative solution.

## 3. The "Impact vs. Effort" Matrix (The Analytical)
**Best for:** Technical or product-heavy roles where data wins arguments.
* **Quantify the Conflict:** "Team A's project had a projected \$50k ROI; Team B's project mitigated a \$200k risk."
* **The Framework:** Explain how you used a 2x2 matrix to plot both priorities.
* **Alignment:** "By visualizing the impact, it became clear to Team A that Team B’s task was a 'High Impact/Low Effort' win that needed to happen first."



## 4. The "Common Goal" Framework
**Best for:** High-level leadership or cross-departmental clashes (e.g., Sales vs. Engineering).
* **Zoom Out:** Remind both teams of the company’s North Star metric (e.g., "Customer Retention").
* **Bridge the Gap:** "I showed how Team A's speed and Team B's stability both contributed to Retention."
* **Trade-offs:** Discuss the "Give and Get." Team A gave up their timeline; Team B gave up extra resources to help Team A catch up later.

## 5. The "Five Whys" Framework
**Best for:** Solving the root cause of the conflict so it doesn't happen again.
* **Identify the Friction:** "Two teams were fighting over the same developer's time."
* **Dig Deeper:** Explain how you asked "Why?" until you found the root cause (e.g., poor resource planning at the executive level).
* **Solve the Root:** "I aligned them by creating a shared resource calendar that gave both teams visibility three weeks in advance."
