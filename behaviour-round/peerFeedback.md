# Can you give an example of a time when you had to deliver feedback to a team member or peer? How did you approach it?
Answering the "difficult feedback" question is a classic way for interviewers to test your emotional intelligence (EQ), communication style, and ability to handle conflict.

Here are five frameworks to structure your response and five variations based on common workplace scenarios.

---

## 5 Frameworks for Structuring Your Answer

These frameworks help you organize your thoughts so you don't ramble. While **STAR** is the best for the overall story, the others are perfect for explaining the "How" of the feedback delivery.

| Framework | What it stands for | Best used for... |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **STAR** | Situation, Task, Action, Result | The "Gold Standard" for any behavioral interview question. |
| **SBI** | Situation, Behavior, Impact | Explaining exactly *what* you said to the person. |
| **DESC** | Describe, Express, Specify, Consequences | Addressing recurring behavioral issues or boundary-setting. |
| **Radical Candor** | Care Personally + Challenge Directly | Showing you value the relationship while being honest. |
| **NVC** | Observation, Feeling, Need, Request | De-escalating high-tension or emotional situations. |

---

## 5 Variations of the "Feedback" Answer

### 1. The Technical Oversight (STAR + SBI)
**Scenario:** A peer consistently makes small errors in code or reports that you have to fix.
* **Situation:** We were nearing a major product launch, and I noticed a peer’s documentation had several recurring technical inaccuracies.
* **Task:** I needed to address this without making them feel micromanaged or discouraged during a high-stress period.
* **Action:** I invited them for a quick coffee. I used the **SBI** model: "In the last three reports (**S**), I noticed the data points for X were unverified (**B**), which led to the stakeholders questioning our credibility (**I**)."
* **Result:** They hadn't realized they were using an outdated source. They updated their process, and our final launch went through without a single data error.

### 2. The Meeting Interrupter (DESC)
**Scenario:** A teammate frequently interrupts others, stifling diverse input.
* **Situation:** During our weekly brainstorms, a senior colleague often talked over junior members.
* **Task:** I wanted to ensure the whole team felt heard while maintaining a good relationship with the senior peer.
* **Action:** I spoke to them privately using the **DESC** script. I **described** the behavior (interrupting), **expressed** that it was causing quieter members to stop contributing, **specified** that we should try a "round-robin" format, and explained the **consequences** (better ideas for the project).
* **Result:** They were genuinely unaware of the habit and thanked me. They actually became the person who enforced the new format going forward.

### 3. The Missed Deadline (Radical Candor)
**Scenario:** A reliable teammate starts slipping on deadlines, affecting your work.
* **Situation:** My project partner missed two consecutive milestones, which put my own deliverables at risk.
* **Task:** I needed to get the project back on track while checking in on their well-being.
* **Action:** I chose the **Radical Candor** approach. I started by saying I valued their work and noticed this was unlike them (Caring Personally). Then, I told them directly that the delays were stalling the project and asked what was causing the bottleneck (Challenging Directly).
* **Result:** It turned out they were overwhelmed with a secondary project. We re-allocated some tasks, and they met every subsequent deadline.

### 4. The Upward/Lateral "Tone" Issue (NVC)
**Scenario:** A peer uses a condescending tone in emails or Slack.
* **Situation:** A peer from another department was using very blunt, almost aggressive language in our shared Slack channel.
* **Task:** Address the communication style without starting a "flame war."
* **Action:** I used **Nonviolent Communication**. I told them, "When I see phrases like 'this makes no sense' in the chat (**Observation**), I feel concerned about our team's morale (**Feeling**). I need us to maintain a collaborative environment (**Need**). Could we try phrasing critiques as questions instead? (**Request**)."
* **Result:** The peer apologized and explained they were just trying to be "efficient." The tone of the channel improved immediately.

### 5. The "Good to Great" Feedback (GROW)
**Scenario:** A team member is doing "fine," but you see potential for them to be "excellent."
* **Situation:** I was mentoring a junior designer who was producing solid work but wasn't presenting their ideas confidently to clients.
* **Task:** Provide feedback that encouraged growth rather than correcting a mistake.
* **Action:** I used the **GROW** (Goal, Reality, Options, Will) model. I asked what their career goals were, discussed the reality of their current presentation style, explored options for improvement (like a public speaking workshop), and established the "Will" (the commitment to lead the next meeting).
* **Result:** They led the next client pitch successfully and were promoted within six months.

---

> **Pro Tip:** When answering this in an interview, always emphasize that you delivered the feedback **in private** and **promptly**. Waiting too long or doing it in front of others is a red flag for recruiters.
